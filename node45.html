Time-dependent deformation: Visco-elasticity

So far, we have assumed that rock strain-stress response is independent of loading rate, however, this is not true. The stiffness of the rock will not be the same if loaded in a time frame of thousands of years (geological time), tens of years (reservoir production time), or during a few minutes (drilling and laboratory time). Rocks tend to be softer and more ductile as the loading time frame increases.

Another expression of visco-elasticity is time-dependent deformation usually known as creep. Whenever a change of stress is applied and set constant, deformations may continue with time. An example of this type of response is when drilling through salt rocks. Stresses intensify around the wellbore after the hole is bored. The wellbore will deform if left uncased and stick to the drilling string.

Figure 3.21: Time-dependent deformation: Creep [Put your own data]

One other expression of visco-elasticity is time-dependent stress change or stress relaxation. Whenever a change of strain is applied and set constant, stresses may relax with time. Unconsolidated sands may relax horizontal stresses with time when a tectonic stress is applied. Therefore, neglecting visco-elasticity may result in overestimations of horizontal stresses in unconsolidated sands with a purely elastic model.
